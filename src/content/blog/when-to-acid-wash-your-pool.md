Pool owners often ask about acid washing as a fix for stained, rough, or discolored pool surfaces. It's one of the most misunderstood pool services — sometimes the right answer, sometimes completely the wrong one. Here's what you need to know.

## What is Pool Acid Washing?

An acid wash (also called a drain and acid wash) is a process that involves:

1. Draining the pool completely
2. Applying diluted muriatic acid to the plaster surface
3. Scrubbing and rinsing systematically to remove staining and a thin layer of plaster
4. Neutralizing and rinsing the surface
5. Refilling and rebalancing

The acid removes organic staining (algae, leaves, debris), mineral staining (calcium, iron, copper), and brightens the plaster surface by etching away the discolored outer layer.

**Critical detail:** Every acid wash removes a small amount of plaster. This can't be undone. A pool that has been acid washed multiple times has progressively thinner plaster — and eventually, there's not enough left to wash again.

## When Acid Washing is Appropriate

An acid wash is the right choice when:

### 1. Severe Algae Contamination
If your pool has been neglected for an extended period — months of zero service — algae can stain the plaster surface deeply enough that chlorine treatment alone won't restore clarity. An acid wash strips the stained layer and gives you a fresh surface to work with.

This is different from a green pool that's been neglected for a few weeks. A proper shock treatment and filtration cycle handles most algae situations without draining. True severe contamination (the water is actually black, multiple failed treatment attempts, visible algae embedded in the surface texture) is when draining and washing makes sense.

### 2. Organic Staining From Debris
Leaves and organic material that sit on the pool bottom for extended periods can leave brown or black stains embedded in the plaster. If the stains are localized and organic in nature, acid washing often removes them successfully.

### 3. Pre-Season Restoration After Neglect
A pool that was poorly managed over a winter or left without service during a period of vacancy sometimes needs a fresh start. Acid washing can restore the surface before the new season.

## When Acid Washing is NOT Appropriate

This is where many pool owners — and some contractors — make mistakes.

### When the Plaster is Already Too Thin
If your pool has been acid washed multiple times (especially within the last 5-7 years), another wash may remove too much material, leaving a compromised, rough, easily-stained surface. Before authorizing a wash, a professional should assess plaster thickness and condition.

### When the Staining is Mineral-Based
Calcium scale (white or off-white crusty deposits) responds to acid washing, but severe calcium buildup on tile and surfaces may indicate a harder water chemistry problem that will just return after washing if not addressed. Iron staining (rust-orange or brown marks) and copper staining (blue-green marks) may require specialized stain removers rather than standard acid washing.

### When Roughness is the Primary Complaint
If your plaster feels rough or sandpaper-like and isn't deeply stained, acid washing won't improve the texture — it will just remove more plaster. Rough plaster that's been etched by years of chemical imbalance or normal wear needs replastering, not washing.

### When the Plaster Shows Structural Issues
Chipping, delaminating, hollow spots, and pitting indicate plaster failure that acid washing will only make worse. These surfaces need replastering.

## How to Tell If You Need Replastering Instead

Replastering is the right answer when:

- Plaster is visibly rough and feels like sandpaper or concrete
- You can see pitting, chipping, or sections that feel hollow when tapped
- The surface has been acid washed more than 2-3 times in its lifetime
- Staining is embedded deeply into the plaster matrix (not on the surface)
- The plaster is 10+ years old with any of the above symptoms
- You're noticing small pieces of plaster or "plaster dust" in the pool water

Replastering is a more significant investment (typically $4,000-$8,000+ depending on finish choice) but provides a 15-20 year fresh start with a premium finish like Stonescapes, versus the 6-12 months of improved appearance you might get from acid washing an already-compromised surface.

## The Chemistry Connection

Both acid washing and replastering address symptoms. If the root cause — water chemistry — isn't fixed, you'll be back in the same situation within a few years.

The most common chemistry-related causes of plaster deterioration in Midlothian pools:

1. **Chronically low pH** — Acidic water etches plaster aggressively. A pool running at pH 7.0 for a year does more damage than a normal lifetime of use.

2. **High calcium hardness** — Our local water is often high in calcium. Combined with high pH, this causes scale deposits that bond to plaster and tile.

3. **Improper startup chemistry** — New plaster is particularly vulnerable in the first 28 days. Incorrect startup chemistry causes permanent surface damage that can't be corrected.
