Pool chemistry is the foundation of every other aspect of pool maintenance. Get the chemistry right, and your water stays clear, your equipment lasts longer, and your family swims in a healthy environment. Get it wrong, and you're fighting algae, etching your plaster, burning through expensive equipment, and spending your weekends troubleshooting instead of swimming.

This guide covers the essential parameters every Texas pool owner should understand, the ideal ranges for our climate, and what happens when things drift out of balance.

## The Core Parameters

### 1. Free Chlorine (FC)
**Ideal range: 1-3 ppm**

Free chlorine is the active sanitizer in your pool. It kills bacteria, viruses, and algae. This is the number you're testing when you check the water.

In Texas, free chlorine burns off fast — especially in summer. Ultraviolet radiation from the sun destroys chlorine, and heat accelerates the process. Without a stabilizer (see below), you can lose nearly all your chlorine in a few hours of direct sunlight.

**Too low (below 1 ppm):** Algae grows, bacteria multiply, water becomes unsafe to swim in.
**Too high (above 5 ppm):** Skin and eye irritation, bleaching of swimwear, can be corrosive to equipment.

### 2. pH
**Ideal range: 7.4-7.6**

pH is the measure of how acidic or alkaline your water is. It directly affects chlorine's effectiveness, your swimmer's comfort, and your pool's surfaces and equipment.

This is the most important parameter to keep in range, because pH affects everything else.

**Too low (below 7.2):** Water becomes corrosive — etches plaster, corrodes metal fittings, causes skin and eye irritation. Also causes chlorine to "burn off" faster.
**Too high (above 7.8):** Chlorine becomes largely ineffective (at pH 8.0, less than 3% of chlorine is active). Scale forms on surfaces and equipment. Water becomes cloudy.

Texas pools tend to drift toward higher pH over time due to our alkaline fill water, aeration from fountains and waterfalls, and carbon dioxide outgassing.

### 3. Total Alkalinity (TA)
**Ideal range: 80-120 ppm**

Total alkalinity acts as a buffer for pH — it's the water's ability to resist pH changes. Think of it as the shock absorber for your pH level.

**Too low (below 60 ppm):** pH becomes unstable and bounces around unpredictably. Small additions of acid or base cause large swings.
**Too high (above 180 ppm):** pH tends to drift upward and resist correction. Can cause cloudiness and scale.

### 4. Cyanuric Acid / Stabilizer (CYA)
**Ideal range: 30-50 ppm**

Cyanuric acid (also called "conditioner" or "stabilizer") protects chlorine from ultraviolet degradation. Without it, outdoor pools in Texas would need constant chlorine additions just to maintain basic sanitization. It's a critically important chemical — but it can cause serious problems when it accumulates too high.

**Too low (below 20 ppm):** Chlorine burns off rapidly in sunlight. Constant additions needed. Expensive and ineffective.
**Too high (above 80 ppm):** Chlorine becomes increasingly ineffective — this is called "chlorine lock." The chlorine is present, but it can't kill algae and bacteria efficiently. This is extremely common in Texas pools that have used trichlor tablets for years.

The only way to lower cyanuric acid is to dilute the pool water (partially drain and refill). There is no chemical that removes it.

### 5. Calcium Hardness (CH)
**Ideal range: 200-400 ppm**

Calcium hardness measures the concentration of calcium ions in your water. The right level keeps water from being corrosive or scale-forming.

**Too low (below 150 ppm):** Water is corrosive and will leach calcium from your plaster, causing pitting and deterioration over time.
**Too high (above 500 ppm):** Scale forms on pool surfaces, tile, and equipment. Water becomes cloudy.

Texas tap water is often high in calcium, so Midlothian-area pools tend to trend toward the higher end of the scale over time.

### 6. Total Dissolved Solids (TDS)
Over time, dissolved chemicals accumulate in pool water that can't be filtered out. When TDS climbs very high (above 2,000-3,000 ppm in fresh water pools), it can affect water clarity and chemical efficiency. The solution is partial or complete draining and refilling.

## How the Parameters Interact: The Langelier Saturation Index

Professional pool technicians use the Langelier Saturation Index (LSI) to determine whether water is corrosive, balanced, or scale-forming. The LSI takes into account pH, alkalinity, calcium hardness, temperature, and TDS together — because these parameters don't affect your pool in isolation.

This is why chasing one number without considering the others leads to problems. Raising alkalinity affects pH. Raising calcium hardness can affect scaling tendency even if pH looks fine.

## Common Texas-Specific Chemistry Challenges

### Phosphates
Dallas-Fort Worth area tap water frequently contains elevated phosphates from municipal water treatment. Phosphates are algae food — they don't directly harm water quality, but they fuel algae growth even when chlorine is adequate. If you're fighting persistent algae despite seemingly correct chemistry, phosphate levels may be the hidden culprit.

### High Calcium Fill Water
Our local water is relatively hard, meaning new water added during refills brings in additional calcium. Over time, this accumulates and contributes to scale on tile and equipment.

### Extreme Summer Heat
Water at 90°F demands much more chlorine than water at 75°F. Dosing schedules that work in spring need to be increased significantly in July and August.

## Testing Frequency

**Weekly at minimum:** Free chlorine and pH. These change fastest and have the most immediate impact.

**Monthly:** Total alkalinity, cyanuric acid, calcium hardness. These are more stable but drift over time.

**Every 3-6 months:** Full comprehensive test including phosphates and TDS.

For the most accurate results, use a professional DPD reagent test kit or bring a water sample to a pool supply store for computerized analysis. Strip tests are convenient but significantly less accurate.

## Getting Help

If your pool chemistry is consistently out of balance despite regular additions, there's almost always a root cause that goes beyond simply adding more chemicals. Common underlying issues include:

- Cyanuric acid that has accumulated too high over years of tablet use
- A malfunctioning chlorinator or salt cell that isn't producing adequate chlorine
- A filter that's not running long enough to properly circulate chemicals
- High phosphate levels fueling algae that consumes chlorine
- Water that needs to be partially replaced to reset TDS levels
