There's nothing more frustrating than walking out to your backyard and finding your pool has gone from crystal clear to swamp green. It happens fast — sometimes within 24 to 48 hours — and it's one of the most common calls we get from pool owners across Midlothian and Ellis County. The good news: green pools are fixable. The better news: they're almost entirely preventable with the right maintenance approach.

## Why Did My Pool Turn Green?

Green pool water is caused by algae — specifically green algae, the most common type in Texas pools. But algae doesn't just appear out of nowhere. It needs a specific set of conditions to bloom, and almost always, those conditions come down to one of these root causes:

### 1. Low or Zero Free Chlorine
This is the number one cause. Chlorine is your pool's primary defense against algae. When free chlorine drops below 1 ppm, algae can colonize quickly. In Texas summer heat, chlorine can drop from 3 ppm to zero in less than 24 hours if stabilizer levels are off or the pool isn't being serviced regularly.

### 2. High Cyanuric Acid (Stabilizer) Levels
Cyanuric acid protects chlorine from UV degradation — a good thing. But when stabilizer levels climb above 80-100 ppm, it begins blocking chlorine's effectiveness so severely that even properly dosed chlorine can't kill algae. This is called chlorine lock. Many pools in Midlothian accumulate high cyanuric acid over time from using stabilized chlorine tablets (trichlor) without diluting the pool.

### 3. Poor pH Balance
Chlorine's effectiveness is directly tied to pH. At a pH of 8.0, only about 3% of your chlorine is in its active killing form. At 7.4-7.6 (the ideal range), about 50-60% is active. A pool with pH that's crept up to 8.2 or higher can have perfectly adequate chlorine readings on paper but almost no actual sanitizing power.

### 4. Insufficient Filtration Run Time
Your pool pump needs to run long enough to turn over the entire volume of water at least once per day — typically 8-12 hours in summer. If your pump is running 4-6 hours and your pool is large, circulation may not be sufficient to distribute chemicals or remove debris effectively.

### 5. Equipment Failure
A failed pump, clogged filter, or malfunctioning chlorinator stops your pool's chemistry and circulation almost immediately. Without knowing the equipment has failed, you can go from healthy water to a green pool very quickly — especially during peak summer heat.

## The Proper Green Pool Treatment Sequence

If your pool is already green, here's the correct treatment approach. Note that doing these steps out of order significantly reduces effectiveness:

### Step 1: Test the Water First
Before adding anything, test your water. You need to know:
- Current chlorine level
- pH level
- Alkalinity
- Cyanuric acid level

If your cyanuric acid is above 80 ppm, you have a dilution problem that shock treatment alone won't solve. No amount of chlorine will clear a pool with extremely high stabilizer levels.

### Step 2: Adjust pH
Shock treatment only works efficiently at pH 7.2-7.4. If your pH is high (above 7.6), lower it with muriatic acid before shocking. If you shock first and then adjust pH, you've wasted a significant portion of your shock.

### Step 3: Shock the Pool
Shock treatment uses a massive dose of chlorine (typically 10x normal levels) to overwhelm and kill the algae. For a heavily green pool, you may need to shock at 3-4 times the standard shocking dose. Use calcium hypochlorite (cal-hypo) shock for best results — it works fast and doesn't add stabilizer to an already-stabilized pool.

Apply shock in the evening or at night. Sunlight can burn off half your shock before it has a chance to work.

### Step 4: Run the Filter Continuously
After shocking, run your filter 24 hours a day. As algae dies, it turns white or grey and needs to be filtered out. You'll need to clean your filter several times during recovery — a clogged filter stops the process.

### Step 5: Brush Thoroughly and Repeatedly
Brush every surface — walls, steps, corners, and the floor — to break algae's grip on the surfaces and push it into the water column where it can be killed and filtered. This step is what most DIY attempts miss.

### Step 6: Retest and Rebalance
Once the water is clearing (usually 2-5 days), retest everything and bring all levels into proper range. Then vacuum up the dead algae that's settled on the bottom.

## How Long Does Green Pool Recovery Take?

For a typical green pool (light to moderate algae bloom), professional treatment restores clear water within 3-5 days. Severe cases — pools that have been neglected for weeks or that have black algae — can take 5-7 days or more.

Variables that affect recovery time:
- Severity of the bloom (light green vs. pea soup vs. black algae)
- Filter type and condition
- Pool size and volume
- Whether root causes are addressed
- Temperature (hotter water means faster die-off but also faster algae regrowth if chemistry slips)

## Preventing Green Pool From Returning

Once you've recovered your pool, the goal is never going through this again. Here's what prevents algae:

1. **Weekly professional maintenance** — The most reliable prevention. Consistent chemistry, regular brushing, and equipment monitoring stop algae before it starts.

2. **Correct stabilizer levels** — Keep cyanuric acid between 30-50 ppm. If it creeps above 80 ppm, partially draining and refilling the pool is often the only solution.

3. **Adequate filtration run time** — 8-10 hours minimum in summer, adjusted for your pool's volume and pump flow rate.

4. **Phosphate management** — Phosphates are algae food. High phosphate levels from leaves, fertilizer runoff, and municipal water treatment make algae blooms more likely. A professional water test will flag this.

5. **Algaecide as a preventive** — Some pool owners use a weekly algaecide dose as added insurance during peak algae season. Ask your pool technician if this makes sense for your pool.

## When to Call a Professional

If you've tried to treat a green pool yourself and haven't seen significant improvement within 3 days, it's time to call a professional. Signs that DIY isn't working:
- The pool is still deeply green or getting worse after shock treatment
- You've shocked multiple times with no results
- The water is clearing but algae keeps returning within days
- You suspect black algae (dark spots embedded in plaster)

Black algae in particular is not a DIY fix. Its protective outer layer resists normal shock treatment, and eliminating it requires physical brushing with specialized tools plus targeted treatment — improper treatment just makes it angrier.
