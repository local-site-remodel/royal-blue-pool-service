If you've moved to Texas from a northern state, you might be expecting to "close" your pool for winter — drain most of the water, add antifreeze, cap the returns, and forget about it until spring. That's what they do in Wisconsin or Ohio. In Texas, that approach is almost never necessary and can actually cause problems.

Here's what pool care in Midlothian and Ellis County looks like from November through March.

## Do We Even Winterize Pools in Texas?

Not in the traditional sense. Here's why:

Texas winters are too mild for full pool closure. Our average low temperatures in Midlothian from November through February stay well above freezing most days. True hard freezes (sustained temperatures below 28°F for multiple hours) are relatively rare events — maybe 2-5 times per year, and usually lasting less than 48 hours.

Full winterization (draining the pool below the returns, plugging lines, covering the pool) creates more problems than it solves in our climate:

1. **Algae** — An empty or nearly empty pool without circulation is an algae incubator. Even in cool weather, a stagnant pool turns green.
2. **Debris** — A covered or neglected pool accumulates debris that's much harder to deal with in spring.
3. **Equipment** — Sitting idle for months can cause seals to dry out and pumps to seize.
4. **Chemical complexity** — Reopening a fully winterized pool is a significant project requiring extensive treatment.

The Texas approach is simpler: **keep the pool running year-round with adjusted chemistry and pump timing**.

## What Texas Pool Owners Should Actually Do in Winter

### 1. Keep Running the Pump (Just Less)
In summer, your pump should run 8-10 hours daily. In winter, you can reduce this to 4-6 hours on mild days, but keep it running. On any day where temperatures are forecast to drop below 35°F overnight, run the pump continuously.

Moving water doesn't freeze. A running pump protects your plumbing, equipment, and water from freeze damage far better than any antifreeze or plugging strategy.

Most modern pool automation systems can be programmed to automatically kick on the pump when temperatures approach freezing. If you have this feature, make sure it's enabled before winter.

### 2. Adjust Your Chemical Dosing
Pool chemistry needs in winter differ from summer:
- **Chlorine demand drops** — Cooler water and fewer bathers mean less chlorine is consumed. Reduce tablet feeding or chlorinator output accordingly to avoid over-chlorinating.
- **pH tends to be more stable** — Reduced aeration and cooler temperatures slow the pH drift that's common in summer.
- **Don't let chemistry slide** — Algae grows in cool water too. Keep chlorine above 1 ppm and pH in range year-round.

### 3. Prepare for Freeze Events
When a hard freeze is forecast for Midlothian (25°F or below, sustained for 4+ hours), take these steps:

**Before the freeze:**
- Run your pump continuously through the freeze event
- If you have a gas heater, you can use it to maintain pool temperature and reduce freeze risk
- Remove any equipment that is not freeze-rated from around the pool pad
- Insulate exposed pipes if they're in an unprotected area

**During the freeze:**
- Keep the pump running — do not shut it off
- If your automation system has a freeze protect setting, verify it's active
- If power goes out, this is when problems happen — check your equipment as soon as power is restored

**After the freeze:**
- Inspect all equipment, fittings, and visible plumbing for cracks or leaks before restarting
- A crack in PVC pipe or a split pump housing may not be apparent until water pressure is applied

### 4. Maintain Regular Cleaning
Don't let leaves and debris accumulate through winter. Decomposing organic matter consumes chlorine and feeds algae. Keep skimmer baskets clear and brush the pool walls and floor at least bi-weekly even during the off-season.

### 5. Schedule a Pre-Season Service Before Summer
Late March or early April — before pool season starts in earnest — is the ideal time for:
- A full equipment inspection
- Filter cleaning
- Water chemistry recalibration
- Any repairs that have been deferred

Getting ahead of maintenance before summer means you're swimming immediately when the weather turns warm instead of dealing with opening issues.

## What to Do If Your Pool Freezes

Despite your best precautions, a prolonged power outage combined with an unusually hard freeze can cause damage. Signs of freeze damage:
- Cracked PVC pipes at fittings or joints
- Cracked pump housing or filter body
- Leaks at unions, valves, or equipment connections
- Skimmer body cracked (from water expanding in the skimmer throat)

If you suspect freeze damage, **do not run the pump until you've inspected the equipment.** Running a pump with a cracked housing or broken plumbing can worsen the damage significantly.
