## Step 1: Learn Your Equipment Before Anything Else

Before you touch a chemical, learn how your pool system works. Every pool is different, but the core components are:

### The Pool Pump
The pump circulates water through your filtration system. It should run 8-10 hours per day during summer months. Know how to:
- Turn it on and off manually
- Read the pressure gauge on your filter
- Clear debris from the strainer basket (located at the pump)

### The Filter
You likely have one of three filter types:
- **Cartridge filter** — Has a removable cartridge that you clean by hosing off every 3-6 months
- **Sand filter** — Uses sand to filter water; requires backwashing when pressure climbs 8-10 PSI above the baseline
- **DE filter** — Uses diatomaceous earth powder; requires backwashing and recharging with fresh DE

Your filter has a pressure gauge. Learn what the "clean baseline" reading is (the pressure when everything is clean and running well). When the gauge reads 8-10 PSI above baseline, the filter needs cleaning.

### The Skimmer
The skimmer is the opening in the side of your pool that draws water from the surface. Inside is a basket that catches leaves and debris. Empty this basket at least weekly — a clogged skimmer basket can starve your pump of flow and cause damage.

### The Heater (if applicable)
If you have a gas heater or heat pump, learn the control panel. Set the temperature to what you want and leave it — constantly adjusting the heater is wasteful and can stress the igniter.

### Automatic Cleaner (if applicable)
Many pools have a Polaris or robotic cleaner. Understand how to connect, disconnect, clean the bag/filter, and adjust the settings. Polaris pressure-side cleaners need their bags emptied regularly.

## Step 2: Understand Your Water Chemistry Basics

You don't need to become a chemist, but you need to understand the six key numbers:

1. **Free chlorine (1-3 ppm):** Your sanitizer. Test this at minimum twice a week in summer.
2. **pH (7.4-7.6):** The most important balance number. Affects everything.
3. **Total alkalinity (80-120 ppm):** Stabilizes pH. Test monthly.
4. **Cyanuric acid/stabilizer (30-50 ppm):** Protects chlorine from sunlight. Test monthly.
5. **Calcium hardness (200-400 ppm):** Prevents corrosive or scale-forming water. Test monthly.
6. **Phosphates:** Algae food. Test every 3-6 months.

Buy a quality liquid test kit — not the cheap strip tests. For the most accurate readings, bring a water sample to a pool supply store once a month for full computerized analysis.

## Step 3: Establish a Maintenance Routine

Here's a realistic weekly routine for a Midlothian pool owner:

**Every day:**
- Make sure your pump ran its scheduled hours
- Glance at the water — is it clear?

**2-3 times per week:**
- Check skimmer basket and empty if needed
- Quick visual check of pump strainer basket

**Weekly:**
- Test chlorine and pH, adjust if needed
- Skim the surface if the automatic cleaner hasn't kept up
- Brush the walls quickly — takes 5 minutes

**Monthly:**
- Full water test (all parameters)
- Inspect and clean cartridge filter if pressure is elevated
- Check all equipment for unusual sounds or leaks

**Every 3-6 months:**
- Professional filter cleaning
- Comprehensive equipment inspection

## Step 4: Know What to Watch For

These are the warning signs that something is wrong and you should call a professional:

**Water-related:**
- Water turning cloudy and not clearing up with normal treatment
- Green or yellowish water
- Strong chlorine smell (paradoxically often means not enough active chlorine)
- Burning eyes or skin irritation after swimming

**Equipment-related:**
- Unusual noises from the pump (grinding, screeching, rattling)
- Pump running but weak water return from jets
- Filter pressure gauge stuck or reading abnormally high/low
- Heater not reaching set temperature

**Structural:**
- Water level dropping faster than normal (possible leak)
- New cracks on pool deck or coping
- Tiles coming loose

## Step 5: Understand Your Chemical Options

At your local pool supply store, you'll find dozens of products. The essentials are:

**Chlorine:** Available as tablets (trichlor, slow-dissolving), liquid (sodium hypochlorite), and granular shock (calcium hypochlorite). Tablets for regular maintenance, liquid or shock for dosing adjustments.

**pH Decreaser (Acid):** Muriatic acid or dry acid (sodium bisulfate). Used when pH drifts above 7.6.

**pH Increaser:** Soda ash. Used when pH drops below 7.2.

**Alkalinity Increaser:** Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate). Used when alkalinity drops below 80 ppm.

**Calcium Hardness Increaser:** Calcium chloride. Used when hardness drops below 200 ppm.

A word of caution: never mix chemicals directly together, and never add chemicals to the pool at the same time. Add one chemical, run the pump for at least 30 minutes, then test and add the next if needed.

Some pool owners manage their own maintenance successfully for years. Others find that the time investment, learning curve, and occasional chemistry crisis isn't worth it. Signs that professional weekly service makes sense for you:

- You travel frequently or have a busy schedule
- You've had recurring algae or chemistry problems

- Your pool has complex equipment (salt system, automation, heater, spa)
