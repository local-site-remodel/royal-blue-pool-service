The pool pump is the heart of your pool's circulation system. When it starts making strange noises, loses pressure, or stops working entirely, you're facing a decision that most pool owners find confusing: repair the existing pump, or replace it with something new?

Having repaired and replaced hundreds of pool pumps across Midlothian and Ellis County over the past 20 years, I can tell you there's no single right answer — but there is a clear framework for thinking through the decision. Here's what I walk every customer through.

## First: Diagnose the Actual Problem

Before you can make an informed repair-vs-replace decision, you need to know what's actually wrong. Common pool pump problems and their typical repair costs:

| Problem | Typical Repair Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Worn shaft seal | $75-$150 | Very common, easy to repair |
| Capacitor failure | $50-$100 | Common on older single-speed pumps |
| Impeller clog or damage | $100-$200 | Often caused by debris bypass |
| Motor bearing failure | $150-$300 | Grinding/screeching sound |
| Motor winding failure | $200-$400 | Burning smell, trips breaker |
| Pump basket cracked | $40-$80 | Simple replacement |

Misdiagnosis is the biggest driver of unnecessary replacement. If a technician says "you need a new pump" without diagnosing the specific component that failed, get a second opinion.

## The Age Question

Pump age is the single most important factor in the repair-vs-replace decision:

**Under 5 years old:** Almost always worth repairing unless there's catastrophic motor failure. A quality pump should last 10-15 years with proper maintenance.

**5-8 years old:** Depends on the type of pump and the repair cost. If the repair is under $200, usually worth doing. If it's a major motor job on a single-speed pump, replacement may make more sense.

**8+ years old (single-speed pump):** This is where the calculus changes significantly. Single-speed pumps are energy hogs — they run at full power 100% of the time. At this age, the efficiency argument for upgrading to variable-speed is compelling.

**8+ years old (variable-speed pump):** Even here, repair is often still worthwhile. Quality variable-speed pumps from Pentair and other manufacturers can last 15-20 years with proper maintenance. A $200-$400 repair on a pump that would cost $800-$1,500 to replace is usually the right call.

## The Variable-Speed Upgrade Argument

If you have an older single-speed pump, a failure event is actually an opportunity. Here's why:

Single-speed pumps run at a fixed RPM — typically 3,450 RPM — all the time. Variable-speed pumps can throttle down to 1,100-1,500 RPM for daily circulation, only ramping up when needed for vacuuming, backwashing, or heating.

The energy savings are substantial. Running at half speed uses roughly 1/8th the energy (power scales as the cube of speed change). A typical Midlothian pool can save $600-$900 annually in electricity by switching from single-speed to variable-speed operation.

At that savings rate, a variable-speed pump upgrade ($800-$1,500 installed) typically pays for itself within 2 years, and then continues saving money for the remaining 12-18 years of service life.

There's also a regulatory angle: Texas and many municipalities are phasing out single-speed pumps above 1 horsepower for residential pools. A failure event may actually be the push you needed to get ahead of future requirements.

What warranty typically doesn't cover:
- Damage from running the pump dry
- Damage from improper installation
- Normal wear and tear on seals and o-rings
- Physical damage from debris

## Signs Your Pump Probably Needs Replacement (Not Repair)

There are situations where replacement clearly wins over repair:

1. **The repair cost exceeds 60% of replacement cost** — If repairing a pump will cost $700 and a new pump is $1,000, the repair doesn't make economic sense unless the pump is relatively new.

2. **Multiple failures in a short period** — If you've repaired the same pump twice in 18 months, the pump is at end of life.

3. **Parts unavailability** — Older pumps (15+ years) sometimes have discontinued parts. If we can't source a proper replacement seal or impeller, repair isn't an option.

4. **The pump is undersized for your pool** — If your pool was expanded, a spa was added, or you installed a water feature that your current pump can't properly circulate, replacement with a correctly sized unit is the right call.

5. **You're upgrading from single-speed and the pump is already struggling** — A struggling old single-speed pump on a replacement-age pool is a strong argument for upgrading.
